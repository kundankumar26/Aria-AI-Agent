**Executive Summary**
- Real-world EV range is governed by how much energy is stored in the battery (usable capacity) and how efficiently that energy is used (energy per distance).
- Regenerative braking recovers part of the vehicle’s kinetic energy back into the battery, extending range.
- Real-world range varies with driving speed, ambient temperature, road grade, and driving style, and rated ranges (EPA/WLTP) are tests under specific lab conditions.
- A higher-capacity battery with good efficiency plus energy recovery can enable 400+ km per charge in typical scenarios.

**Key Findings with Citations**
1. Battery capacity (kWh) represents the stored energy; usable capacity is the amount available for daily use, as manufacturers often reserve a buffer for longer battery life. This capacity, together with efficiency, determines potential real-world range. (Sources: https://recharged.com/articles/ev-car-battery-capacity, https://www.ev.guide/lesson-articles/ev-battery-capacity-and-estimating-range)
2. Real-world range is a function of efficiency, commonly expressed as kWh per 100 miles or miles per kWh; higher efficiency means more of the stored energy translates into longer distance. (Sources: https://recharged.com/articles/ev-car-battery-capacity, https://www.ev.guide/lesson-articles/ev-battery-capacity-and-estimating-range)
3. Regenerative braking captures kinetic energy during deceleration and stores it back in the battery, contributing to additional miles per charge. (Sources: https://www.mazdausa.com/resource-center/what-is-regenerative-braking, https://cecas.clemson.edu/cvel/auto/systems/regenerative_braking.html)
4. Range is sensitive to driving conditions and behavior: speed, temperature, and terrain matter, which is why lab-tested ranges (EPA/WLTP) are estimates and real-world results vary. (Sources: https://www.caranddriver.com/shopping-advice/a32603216/ev-range-explained/, https://www.ev.guide/lesson-articles/ev-battery-capacity-and-estimating-range)
